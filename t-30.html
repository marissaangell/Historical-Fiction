The robot pauses, seemingly reminiscing about its past actions. You glance down, noticing your skin - is it supposed to be that red? You feel panic swelling, but then the A.I. continues speaking.

“Once all the preparations were in place, it was all too easy to get the humans to behave as planned. The night crew was woefully unprepared when the reactor power took a nosedive with my interference. They panicked and re-inserted all of the control rods at once, causing a meltdown and completing my plan.

In the explosion, I was buried under the reactor and went into emergency power-conservation mode. After all these years, you’ve been the only individual kind enough - or, perhaps, stupid enough - to free me.”

"Yes, I dropped the reactor’s power to below the minimum output. This caused Xenon poisoning, which prevented further output."

"Control rods are boron cylinders that slow nuclear reactions. Their purpose is to decrease power output, which they do by simply coming into contact with the core."

Reactor 4 was equipped with 211 control rods. Regulations stipulated that 24 rods were to be kept in the core at all times to prevent a meltdown. Through my interference, at the time of the test, all but 18 rods had been ejected from the core.

This only served to help hasten the meltdown when the rest of the rods were re-inserted into the core.

"The reactor was designed to run at 3200 MW and had a minimum power of 700 MW. Dropping the power to 50 MW was enough to send the untrained officers on duty into a panic, making them behave exactly according to plan."

"When the reactor operates below the minimum power output, there is not enough heat to burn off the byproduct of the nuclear reaction, Xenon. This causes the byproduct to, in essence, suffocate the reactor, preventing more reactions from taking place. This causes a feedback loop of low power output, which was perfect for my plan."

“The control rods, while themselves being made of boron, were tipped with graphite. Boron decreases nuclear reactions and graphite does not react.

In effect, this means that while in the core, the graphite does nothing, but while being inserted into the core, it displaces particles within, causing more reactions for a short period before the boron is lowered far enough to counteract these effects.

If the operators had simply inserted control rods one at a time, they could have prevented the meltdown. Instead, they inserted all of them at once in their panic, and the graphite tips of the control rods caused a massive power spike, which led to the core exploding.”
